# Core considerations

How do we clearly reach Americans who are frustrated with government and are open to constitutional principles as the standard for political accountability?

Americans who believe government should be limited by the Constitution and want to know whether their elected officials actually vote that way. BUT...the challenge is to explain this at a 14-year-old level and minimize cognitive load (near zero).


> What problem do people feel we help solve?
“I’m tired of politicians saying whatever it takes to get elected, then voting for a government that keeps getting bigger, more expensive, and less accountable.”

The problem is not: “I need to know whether my legislators honor the Constitution.”
The problem is: “The people in office keep making my life harder, more expensive, and less free — and I don’t know who is actually part of the problem.”
Or: “Politicians say they work for us, but government keeps getting bigger, life keeps getting more expensive, and nothing seems to change.”

We score the votes that matter: Does this give government more power — or leave people more free?

Our standard is the U.S. Constitution — limited government, responsible spending, national sovereignty, and avoiding foreign entanglements.

> The Freedom Index: A Congressional Scorecard Based on the U.S. Constitution” rates members of Congress based on their adherence to constitutional principles of limited government, fiscal responsibility, national sovereignty, and a traditional foreign policy of avoiding foreign entanglements. For information on the criteria we use to select the votes, go to TheFreedomIndex.org/about.

Or the inverse we've been working with:
"Keep government small and your life big."

The real problem set
When people who believe in limited government think about politics, the frustration isn't primarily informational. It's emotional and existential. Here's an honest inventory, roughly in order of intensity:
Betrayal
They voted for someone who said the right things and then governed differently. This isn't disappointment — it's the specific sting of being lied to by someone you trusted. It's personal. It repeats every election cycle. The scar tissue builds up over decades.
Powerlessness
Their vote feels meaningless. The machine keeps growing regardless of who wins. Spending goes up under both parties. Government expands under both parties. The feeling isn't just frustration — it's the creeping suspicion that participation itself is a charade.
The lesser-of-two-evils trap
You identified this early in our conversation and it's the sharpest emotional truth on this list. Every election feels like a choice between two people neither of whom actually represents you. You're not voting for someone. You're voting against someone worse. That's a deeply demoralizing place to live politically.
Being ignored
The Boston Tea Party parallel you drew is exactly right. Taxation without representation wasn't just a tax complaint — it was the rage of people whose voice didn't count. That feeling is alive today. People sense that legislators serve donors, party leadership, lobbyists, and their own careers — in that order. The constituent is last.
The promise-to-reality gap
Campaign mode and governing mode are two completely different performances. People know this viscerally. They've watched it happen too many times to be surprised by it. The frustration isn't shock anymore — it's exhausted cynicism. They expect to be lied to, which is almost worse than the lie itself.
Watching the country change in ways they didn't vote for
This is the biggest and most existential feeling. Spending that their grandchildren will pay. Freedoms quietly eroding through regulation, not legislation. Government inserting itself into decisions that used to belong to individuals and families. The feeling that something is being lost and nobody with power is stopping it.
The absence of a standard
This one is undernamed but important. People sense there's no referee. No objective measure of whether a politician kept their word. It's all spin, counter-spin, and whoever controls the narrative wins. They feel the absence of an objective standard even if they can't name what that standard should be.

The problem people feel is not "I lack information about votes."
The problem is: I keep getting lied to and I can't do anything about it.
The benefit you offer is not "here's how they voted."
The benefit is: now you have proof — and proof changes the equation.
